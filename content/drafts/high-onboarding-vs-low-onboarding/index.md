Some engineers really hate onboarding onto new products. When they change jobs or teams, they grit their teeth through the weeks where they're learning a new codebase, and only start to enjoy their work when they're familiar with the system again. Their ideal job is one where they get to be stewards of the same system for years, which is enough time to develop truly deep expertise. That kind of expertise - where the entire system sits at your fingertips - is the state of nirvana they chase for their whole careers. 

Other engineers only enjoy work if they're onboarding onto someting new. If they have to work on a single system for more than a couple of months, they get bored. Their ideal job is more like consultancy work: constantly being assigned new systems and new problems, expected to ramp up quickly and make an impact before they're moved to the next area. They exchange the feeling of deep expertise in a single domain for the rush of parachuting into brand-new problems, and the satisfying experience of being the "go-to engineer" who can be thrown at hard problems.

Whether you're the first or second kind of engineer[^1] is really just a statement about your personality type. You can be a skilled engineer either way. But **some engineering organizations strongly favor one type or the other**. Some engineering cultures expect engineers to be generalists: able to quickly onboard to new tasks, operate with high agency, and figure out how things work without much hand-holding. Other engineering cultures expect engineers to go extremely deep on the services they own, but in return they typically have long and elaborate onboarding procedures.

One way to tell which organization you're in is to see how much time it takes until a new engineer is expected to be productive. I've worked in teams where it didn't really matter if a new engineer did anything useful at all in their first month, and they weren't expected to lead projects until a year in. I've also worked in teams where new engineers were expected to ship something useful in their first week, and were handed projects to lead very shortly afterwards.
