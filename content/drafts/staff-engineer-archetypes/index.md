The most influential piece of writing about staff engineers in the last decade has to be Will Larson's [_Staff engineer archetypes_](https://staffeng.com/guides/staff-archetypes/). He argues that the "staff engineer" title covers at least four very different roles: the team lead, the architect, the solver, and the right hand.

In [both](/staff-engineer-promotions) of my promotions to staff engineer, my manager at the time linked me to the "staff engineer archetypes" and asked me to consider which of these archetypes I was aiming towards. I can see why this is popular advice, but like most popular advice on how to succeed at a tech company, it isn't very good.

### Archetypes are not good goals

To see why, let's take the "team lead" archetype. Larson describes this as an informal technical leadership role: not necessarily an explicit authority figure, but someone who's good at scoping work, planning projects, and maintaining the kind of relationships (e.g. with other teams) needed to successfully [ship](/how-to-ship). If you want to fill this role, shouldn't you start trying to do these things? No! You don't become a technical leader by trying really hard to be a technical leader, much like you don't become a writer by trying really hard "to be a writer". You become a technical leader by _doing good technical work_ until your skills and relationships emerge organically.

I wrote about this process in [_Ratchet effects determine engineer reputation at large companies_](/ratchet-effects). To get good at shipping large complex projects, you must start by shipping tiny pieces of work, until you're familiar enough with the system and you've built enough trust to take on slightly larger pieces. At each stage, if you do good work - "good work" here means "deliver [shareholder value](/shareholder-value/)" - you will very naturally be given opportunities to work on more complex and important things.

The other archetypes are like this as well. If you want to become a successful architect, you do not get there by studying software architecture in the abstract, because [you can't design software you don't work on](/you-cant-design-software-you-dont-work-on/). The "solver" and "right hand" archetypes both rely on having an enormous amount of trust and influence. You can't aim for those archetypes directly, because trust and influence accumulate over time. In fact, the idea of "aiming for" a particular staff engineer archetype reflects a misunderstanding of what the staff engineer role is.

### What is a staff engineer?

**A staff engineer is useful to the company.** A senior or mid-level software engineer doesn't have to be useful, they simply have to execute on the job in front of them. If they end up not providing value (maybe their project turns out to be unimportant, or they don't get the support needed to succeed) that's their manager's problem, not theirs[^1]. In contrast, staff engineers are expected to deliver value regardless: to make the project work, or to find something else useful to do if the project truly can't be salvaged.

This is an unfair expectation, since often projects really do fail through no fault of your own. In fact, that's a defining feature of the staff engineer role: it's fundamentally _unfair_, in a literal sense. Something many engineers don't realize is that senior management and executive leadership jobs work like this as well. If a company is failing, expect leadership to be purged, whether or not they could have done anything about it. That's just part of the deal: C-staff are given power and great compensation, and in return they will sometimes get thrown out of the boat in bad weather[^2].

If you want to become or be more effective as a staff engineer, you should not start trying to do one of the archetypes. Instead, you should develop the skill **of being useful to the organization** and the tendency **to not complain about fairness**. At the beginning, that won't look anything like the staff engineer archetypes. It will look like being a level-headed engineer who can be trusted to move projects forward with a minimum of fuss, or who can be re-tasked to different work without whining. It will look like someone who's [paying a lot of attention](/getting-the-main-thing-right/) to what their manager's actual priorities are, and who is thinking hard about how to fulfil those priorities (instead of their own goals).

### Final thoughts

None of this is a criticism of Will Larson or the original staff engineer post. Larson is pretty clear that these archetypes are more of an anthropological description of some of the varied niches staff engineers fill, not a how-to guide for succeeding in the role[^3]. I'm criticizing the increasingly common practice of using Larson's archetypes as a how-to guide, which I think is totally backwards.

The staff engineer archetypes are a good list of ways an engineer can be very useful to their organization, once they've built a deep relationship of trust with their organization's leadership. Advice on how to succeed as a staff engineer should be about **how to build that trust**, not about what to do once you have it.
